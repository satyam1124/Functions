Chapter 3: Running Gear

⏱ 25 min read
📚 8 Topics
● Intermediate

The running gear of a marine diesel engine includes all components that convert the reciprocating motion of the piston into the rotary motion of the crankshaft. Key components include the crankshaft (semi-built or fully-built), connecting rod, crosshead (in 2-stroke slow-speed engines), and piston with its rod and rings.


In large 2-stroke crosshead engines like the MAN B&W ME series, the piston rod passes through a stuffing box that separates the scavenge space from the crankcase. This design allows different lubricants for cylinder and crankcase, preventing contamination.

🚢 Real Ship Example: On a MAN B&W 6S50ME-C engine aboard a Panamax bulk carrier, the crankshaft weighs approximately 120 tonnes. During major overhaul at 16,000 running hours, crankshaft deflections are measured to detect misalignment.
Crankshaft types: Semi-built (shrink-fit) most common in large marine engines. Fully-built uses separate webs, pins, journals bolted together.
Connecting rod: Transmits force from crosshead to crankpin. Forged steel construction. Bottom end has white metal or tri-metal bearing shells.
Crosshead purpose: Only in 2-stroke engines. Absorbs side thrust preventing it from acting on cylinder liner. Has guide shoes and pin bearing.
Piston cooling: Large engines use bore cooling or jet/cocktail shaker cooling with system oil or separate cooling oil circuit.
Deflection readings: Measured at 5 positions (TDC, BDC, Port, Starboard, and halfway). Excess deflection indicates misalignment.
