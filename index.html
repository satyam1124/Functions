Chapter 3: Running Gear

25 min read
8 Topics
● Intermediate

The running gear of a marine diesel engine comprises all the components that convert the reciprocating motion of the piston into the rotary motion of the crankshaft. This includes the crankshaft (semi-built or fully-built construction), connecting rod, crosshead (in large 2-stroke engines), piston with its rings and rod, and the stuffing box that seals the scavenge space.


In large slow-speed 2-stroke crosshead engines (like MAN B&W MC/ME series), the piston rod passes through a stuffing box with sealing rings to separate the scavenge space from the crankcase. The crosshead pin and bearing allow the connecting rod to transmit force while the crosshead guides absorb the side thrust.

🚢 Real Ship Example: On a MAN B&W 6S50ME-C engine aboard a bulk carrier, the crankshaft weighs approximately 120 tonnes and is semi-built construction. During a major overhaul at 16,000 running hours, the crosshead bearing is inspected, and crankshaft deflections are measured to check for misalignment.
  • Crankshaft types: Semi-built (shrink-fit) is most common in large marine engines; fully-built uses separate webs, pins, and journals bolted together.
  • Connecting rod: Transmits force from crosshead to crankpin. Made of forged steel. Bottom end has bearing shells (white metal or tri-metal).
  • Crosshead: Only in 2-stroke engines. Absorbs side thrust that would otherwise act on the cylinder liner. Has guide shoes and pin bearing.
  • Piston types: Trunk piston (4-stroke medium-speed) vs crosshead piston (2-stroke slow-speed). Cooling: bore cooling or jet/cocktail shaker cooling.
  • Crankshaft deflection: Measured to detect misalignment. If deflection exceeds class limits, realignment of bearings or engine foundation is required.
